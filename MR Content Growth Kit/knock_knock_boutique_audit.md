---

## Competitive Landscape

Knock Knock sits in a favorable gap in the Hershey market. Its direct competitors are mostly fine jewelry (The Jewelers Bench, Leitzel's, Mountz) or chains (Pandora). Nobody else in this market is doing:
- Accessible, trendy fashion jewelry at $16–$35
- Permanent jewelry booking as a service
- A boutique experience designed for repeat visits and gift-giving

The competitive threat isn't other boutiques — it's that their digital presence undersells a brand that already has loyal locals and a prime tourist location. The store earns the customer. The online presence doesn't.

**Untapped opportunity:** Hersheypark draws millions of visitors annually. Chocolate Avenue is on the tourist path. Knock Knock is not marketing to tourists at all — no "visiting Hershey?" content, no "perfect souvenir" messaging, no permanent jewelry as a "take home a memory from Hershey" angle. That's an entire audience segment being left at the door.

## What's Working

**Owner Emily is a brand asset.**
Multiple reviews mention her by name — "met the owner Emily," "Emily was so helpful." That personal connection is rare and powerful for a small boutique. It's not showing up in their content at all.

**The permanent jewelry service is genuinely differentiating.**
Permanent jewelry (welded on, no clasp) is a growing trend. It's appointment-based, higher perceived value, and extremely social-media-friendly — people document the experience and share it. Knock Knock offers this and it's barely visible on the website or likely in their content.

**Loyal local customer base.**
Reviews say "every time you visit you find something you can't leave without." Repeat purchase behavior is built in. They just need content that reaches new people as effectively as the store retains existing ones.

**Strong review profile.**
4.7 stars, #3 shopping destination in Hershey on TripAdvisor. That's real social proof. None of it appears on the homepage.

**Price point is a conversion advantage.**
$16–$35 for most jewelry is an easy impulse purchase. The website isn't using that to its advantage — there's no "under $25" collection, no "gifts under $30" filter, no urgency around the price point.

## What's Costing Them

**The homepage is built around the wrong product.**
The hero experience leads with Jewel Boxes — jewelry organizers currently on sale at $65 (down from $125). That 48% markdown on the hero product signals clearance, not excitement. A first-time visitor doesn't know what Knock Knock is yet, and the first thing they see is a discounted organizer box.

**Permanent jewelry is buried.**
This is their most unique, most bookable, most content-worthy service and it requires digging to find on the site. Someone searching "permanent jewelry Hershey PA" or "permanent jewelry near me" should land directly on a dedicated page with photos, pricing, and a booking button. That page may not exist.

**The brand positioning is split and confusing.**
The site calls itself "your jewelry on-the-go solution" and "Jewel box is your tool box for fashion emergencies." Is this a jewelry boutique or a jewelry organization product company? Those are two different customers. The boutique — the local store, the trendy pieces, Emily, the experience — is the real brand. The Jewel Box is a product. They're leading with the product and burying the brand.

**Almost no social proof on the website.**
4 reviews shown on a site for a business with 4.7 stars and a loyal following. The reviews they have are strong and specific. They should be everywhere.

**The Instagram content ideas are good. The visual system is broken.**
Live grid review tells the full story. They are posting about permanent jewelry — Valentine's BOGO promo, chain close-ups, a dark editorial "Just one more" post. The owner and staff appear in content regularly, which is their strongest asset. Events are a consistent content pillar: Teal Carpet, Sprinkles & Sparkles, Spring Sparkle Runway, Blind Date with a Book — genuine community involvement that most boutiques don't have.

The problem is that none of it holds together visually. Warm editorial product shots sit next to flat white background photos sit next to bright DIY Canva graphics in teal, hot pink, and lime green. The event promotion graphics especially — "Blind Date with a Book," the Valentine's promo, the Sprinkles & Sparkles flyer — look like a completely different brand. A new visitor scrolling the grid has no idea what Knock Knock looks like because it looks like five things at once.

They're also burning grid space on operational posts — "Closed Monday January 26" takes up a permanent slot in the feed and offers nothing to a new visitor discovering the account.

The instincts are right. The execution needs a visual system and a consistent template for promotions so the grid reads as one brand, not a bulletin board.

**No email incentive.**
The newsletter signup exists but offers nothing. "Sign up for updates" competes with every other inbox ask. A small first-purchase discount or early access to new arrivals would meaningfully increase capture rate.

## 2 Proof-of-Work Exhibits

### Exhibit 1 — Hook + Caption: Permanent Jewelry

**Before (what they likely have):**
*"Permanent jewelry is now available at Knock Knock! Book your appointment today. Link in bio."*

**After (what it should be):**

Hook: "I got welded into my jewelry and I'm never going back."

Caption: Permanent jewelry means no clasp, no taking it off, no losing it. Just a bracelet or necklace that stays with you. We weld it on right here in the shop — takes about 20 minutes and the result is something you'll wear every single day.

Perfect for best friends. Moms and daughters. A solo treat-yourself moment in Hershey.

Book your appointment — link in bio. Spots go fast on weekends.

CTA: "Tag who you'd get matching permanent jewelry with 👇"

---

**Why this is the right exhibit:**
Permanent jewelry is a trend that sells itself visually — people share the moment it happens. One well-written hook turns a service announcement into a shareable experience post. And it directly drives appointment bookings, which is their highest-value conversion.

---

### Exhibit 2 — Brand Direction: Two Concepts

**Direction A: "Local Favorite"**
Leans into the Hershey community, Emily's presence, the loyal regulars, the boutique warmth.
- Palette: Warm terracotta · Cream · Deep chocolate brown
- Font: Friendly rounded serif headline · Clean sans-serif body
- Energy: The kind of shop locals bring visitors to. Warm, personal, unpretentious.
- Logo direction: Knock Knock wordmark with a subtle door/knock graphic mark

**Direction B: "Modern Jewelry Studio"**
Positions the permanent jewelry service as a premium experience, elevating the brand beyond gift shop.
- Palette: Warm gold · Off-white · Near-black
- Font: Elegant thin serif headline · Minimal sans-serif body
- Energy: A boutique that tourists take seriously and locals come back to for occasions.
- Logo direction: KK monogram or a minimal chain link mark

---

**Why two directions:**
Direction A protects what's working — the local loyalty and Emily's brand. Direction B opens the door to the tourist market and positions permanent jewelry as a destination experience, not just a walk-in service. Either direction is stronger than the current brand, and showing both gives Emily a choice, not a prescription.

## 5 Quick Wins

**1. Give permanent jewelry its own page.**
Dedicated URL, photos of the service in action, pricing, and a booking button. Add it to the main nav. This service is bookable and higher ticket — it deserves to be found.

**2. Replace the hero with the brand story.**
Lead with what Knock Knock actually is: a local jewelry boutique with fresh arrivals, permanent jewelry, and a reason to keep coming back. Move the Jewel Box sale to a product collection page.

**3. Add a "Visiting Hershey?" section.**
A simple block targeting tourists: "Taking something home from Hershey? Make it permanent." Link to permanent jewelry booking and gift-ready collections. Chocolate Avenue foot traffic is free advertising — capture it.

**4. Surface the reviews.**
Pull the best 3-4 TripAdvisor and Google reviews onto the homepage. Especially the ones that mention Emily by name, specific products, and repeat visits.

**5. Rewrite the Instagram bio.**
Current (likely): generic boutique description + location + hours
What it should be:
*Trendy jewelry + permanent jewelry studio ✨ Hershey + Elizabethtown, PA · Est. 2014 · Book permanent jewelry → [link]*

## Content Opportunities

**"The Permanent Jewelry Experience"**
Document the process — arrival, choosing the chain, the weld, the finished result. Show real people's reactions. This is one of the most shareable content formats for a jewelry boutique and it drives direct bookings.

**"What's New This Week"**
A weekly new arrivals post. Keeps followers checking back. Reinforces the "every time you visit you find something new" feeling that existing customers already love.

**"Visiting Hershey? You Need to Know About This"**
Tourist-facing content that positions the store as a Hershey must-visit. Especially powerful in summer (Hersheypark season) and holiday season.

**"Gift Under $25"**
A recurring series showing specific giftable items under $25. Captures the easy impulse purchase and drives online orders.
